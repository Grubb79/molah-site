Why learn Molah?

Molah is a new language designed specifically as a lingua franca for trade, travel, and basic cross-cultural communication. Unlike English or other natural languages that evolved over centuries with complex rules and thousands of exceptions, Molah was built from the ground up to be learnable in weeks, not years.

Molah was designed by Santullo, a world traveler and PhD in Communication Studies who has lived in 5 different countries. Because Santullo always struggled with perfecting other languages, he decided it was time to give the world a functional but simple language. In fact, Molah is the world's simplest language!

Whether you're a business traveler, tourist, or someone who wants to communicate across language barriers, Molah gives you the essential tools for mutual understanding.

Overview of the Molah language

Before diving into the lessons, here is a quick map of what makes Molah different from every natural language you may have encountered. Each of these principles will be demonstrated in the lessons that follow.

Easy alphabet — one sound per letter

Molah uses only 16 letters. Every letter makes exactly one sound, always. There are no silent letters, no letters that change sound depending on context, and no combinations that produce unexpected sounds. What you see is what you say.

Simple sentence structure — Subject · Verb · Object

Every Molah sentence follows the same fixed order: who does something, what they do, and what they do it to. Ya bama brim — I (subject) want (verb) drink (object). This order never changes, which means you never have to guess where to put a word.

No tonal structure

In languages like Mandarin or Vietnamese, the pitch of your voice changes the meaning of a word entirely. Molah has no tones — the same word spoken high or low, fast or slow, means the same thing. Only a rising tone at the end of a sentence signals a question, and even that is optional when a question word is present.

No irregular verb forms

In English, go becomes went in the past — a completely different word. In Molah, every verb follows the same rule without exception: add -ko for past, add -tu for future. Bo (go) becomes bo-ko (went) and bo-tu (will go). Always. Every verb. No exceptions.

No articles

English requires you to choose between a, an, and the — and the rules are complex. Molah has no articles at all. Ya bama brim means both "I want a drink" and "I want the drink." Context makes the meaning clear, as it does in most real conversations. But you can also add a word for here/this (Wu) or there/that (Wutu) if needed.

No noun classes

Many languages assign every noun to a gender or class — masculine, feminine, neuter — and this forces every surrounding word (adjectives, articles, verbs) to change its form to match. French, Spanish, German, Arabic, and Swahili all do this. Molah does not. A noun is just a noun. Nothing around it changes because of what kind of thing it is.

Combine or repeat words to make new meanings

Molah builds new concepts from existing ones rather than introducing new vocabulary. Smakkr (make) + Wif (place) = Smakkr-Wif (build). Mor (mother) doubled = Mor-mor (grandmother). Yuhm (good) doubled = Yuhm-yuhm (really good). Once you know the roots, the combinations are transparent.

These seven principles are all you need to understand before starting. Everything else follows naturally from them.

Greetings

Bah-t baht No
Molah! moe-lah I recognize you! / Hello back!

Molah! — the response to a greeting

When someone says Lowi! to you, the natural response is Molah! — the name of the language itself used as a joyful acknowledgment. It carries the feeling of "I see you!", "I know you!", or "We share this!" Like Aloha in Hawaiian or Shalom in Hebrew, the word is bigger than its literal parts. A full greeting exchange goes:

Person A: Lowi! (Hello!)
Person B: Molah! (I recognize you! / Hello back!)

With Kif for formality: Kif Lowi! → Kif Molah!

Bolah explained

Bolah combines Bo (go) with the lah ending (happy/warm). So goodbye in Molah literally means "go happily" — a warm send-off built from words you already know.

Formal respect — Kif

Molah Pronunciation English
Kif keef Formal respect marker

[!] and [~] — Molah's two special sounds

[!] is a tongue click — the only click in Molah — and means "and". It is written as [!] and spoken as a single sharp click of the tongue against the roof of the mouth. Example: Ya bama it [!] brim = I want food and drink.

[~] is made by clenching the teeth and sucking air inward — a reverse "suh" sound. It means "but" / "however" / "0" (zero). Written as [~]. Example: Ya bama it [~] bah-t brim = I want food but not drink. As a number, [~] = zero.

When to use Kif

Place Kif at the start of any sentence to signal deep formal respect — reserved for someone of great importance, wealth, or power. Use it rarely and deliberately. Overusing it weakens its meaning.

Transition words — the Zee- prefix

The prefix Zee- signals a reason or connection between ideas. It is Molah's way of linking clauses and explaining relationships.

Molah Pronunciation English

Zee-bobo zee-boh-boh After / Afterwards (going forward in time)

The Zee- prefix

Zee- marks any word as a connector — something that links what came before to what comes after. points direction (to/toward). Zee-kahb gives a reason (because). Zee-bobo places something in sequence (after). Notice that Zee-bobo doubles Bo (go) — bobo = going-going = continued forward motion = afterwards.

Ya bo musyum.
(yah boh moo-syum)

(yah bah-mah brim zee-kahb yah tah-mee)
I want drink because I am sick.
Mo it zee-bobo mo sily.
(moh it zee-boh-boh moh sih-lee)
We eat, after which we play.

Wu and Wutu

Wu = here/this — close to you. Wutu extends Wu with -tu, already used in Molah to indicate something forward or away (future tense, tomorrow). So wutu = "here but forward" = there/that. One suffix, two uses: time and space.

Lesson 4: Word order (S-V-O)

Molah follows a fixed Subject-Verb-Object word order, just like English. This makes it easy to construct sentences.

The pattern

Subject → Verb → Object
Ya bama brim.
(yah bah-mah brim)
I want drink. (Ya=subject, bama=verb, brim=object)
Yo low ya.
(yoh lohw yah)
You love me. (Yo=subject, low=verb, ya=object)
Mo bo musyum.
(moh boh mu-syum)
We go museum. (Mo=subject, bo=verb, musyum=object)

Modifiers (adjectives)

Adjectives go before nouns:

Bik ham bo.
(bik hahm boh)
Big person goes.
Ya bama yuhm it-yuhm.
(yah bah-mah yoom it-yoom)
I want good food.

Practice: Build sentences

Subject: Ya, Verb: ur, Object: yuhm → Ya ur yuhm (I am good)
Subject: Yo, Verb: sys (see), Object: tamham (thing) → Yo sys tamham
Add "bik" (big) before "tamham": Yo sys bik tamham (You see big thing)

Molah makes tenses incredibly simple: add -ko for past, add -tu for future. No irregular verbs, no exceptions!

Lesson 6: Asking questions

Questions in Molah use simple question words. You can also make any statement into a question just by using a rising tone!

Question words

Molah Pronunciation English
Wu-wu woo-woo Where
Waka wah-kah What, how, which
To-to toh-toh When
Walh wahlh How much (price/value)

Words carry their meaning — by design

Wu, Wu-wu, and the logic of Molah words

In Molah, words are designed to give you a conceptual clue about what they mean — even before you've learned them. Notice that Wu means "here / this" (something close, immediate), and Wu-wu means "where?" Doubling Wu turns a statement of closeness into a question about location — as if you're saying "here... here?" while looking around for something.

The same logic runs through all the question words:

Question word Root idea How it works
Wu-wu — Where? Wu = here / this Doubled: "here... here?" — searching for a place
To-to — When? To = a moment / point Doubled: "now... now?" — searching for a time
Waka — What / How / Which? Wakka = walk / way Which way? What path? — searching for direction or identity
Walh — How much? Wahl = value What is its value? — directly rooted in the concept of worth

This is intentional

Molah was designed so that a new speaker can often make an educated guess at meaning from the sound and shape of a word alone. Wu sounds close and immediate. Wu-wu sounds like a search. Waka sounds like movement. Walh sounds like weighing something. This is not an accident — it is the philosophy of the language.

Question examples

Wu-wu, yo ur?
(woo-woo, yoh ur)
Where are you from?
Waka, yo bama?🔊
(yoh bah-mah wah-kah)
What do you want?

Word order in questions — the full rule

The order is always: Question word → Time word → Subject → Verb → Object

Comma rule: Always place a comma after the opening word(s). After a question word alone: Waka, yo bama? After a time word alone: Zora, mo tafl. After both — comma goes after the time word: Waka zym, tram bo?

Rule 1: Question words (Wu-wu, Waka, To-to, Walh) go first. Waka yo bama? = What do you want? Not Yo bama waka?

Rule 2: If both a question word AND a time word are present, the question word comes first and the time word comes immediately after — then S-V-O. Waka zym, tram bo? = What time does the train go? Not Zym, tram bo waka?

Rule 3: If there is only a time word (no question word), it leads alone. Zora, mo tafl-tu Molah. = Today, we will speak Molah.

Waka zym, tram bo?
(wah-kah zeem, trahm boh)
What time does the train go?
Walh?
(wahlh)
How much?

Practice: Ask questions

Where is toilet? → Wu-wu, Ikrit-wif ur?
When will you go? → To-to, yo bo-tu?
What do I want? → Waka, ya bama?

Lesson 7: Sentence endings and intensification

The "lah" ending

Adding "lah" at the end of a sentence indicates what was said should be understood as happy, cute, or cheeky. It adds a friendly, positive tone.

Ya bo beach lah!
(yah boh beach lah)
I go to beach (happily/excitedly)!
Yo ur yuhm ham lah.
(yoh ur yoom hahm lah)
You are good person (said warmly).

The "bah" ending

Adding "bah" at the end indicates something is not funny, strange, or bad.

Wythar ur bah.
(wy-thar ur bah)
Weather is bad.

Intensification through repetition

Repeating a word shows intensity, age, or depth. This is a key feature of Molah!

Single Repeated (Intensified) Meaning
low low-low love → really love / very much
lah lah-lah happy → very happy/funny
bah bah-bah bad → really bad
woom woom-woom fast → really fast
uhhh uhhh-uhhh slow → very slow
kru kru-kru cry → weep (deep crying)
mor mor-mor mother → grandmother
fah fah-fah father → grandfather

Examples in sentences

Ya low-low yo!
(yah lohw-lohw yoh)
I really love you!
Ham ram woom-woom.
(hahm rahm woom-woom)
He/She runs really fast.
Ya kal-tu mor-mor zora-tu.
(yah kahl-too mor-mor zorah-too)

Dialogue 1: At a shop

You: Yelb! Yo kabah fish?
(Help! Do you know fish?)

Shopkeeper: Low! Mo yabur fish wu.
(Yes! We have fish here. (yabur = have))

Lesson 9: The 80 core words of Molah

Why only 80 core words?

Molah's 80 core words are carefully chosen to cover all essential daily communication. Do not add new verbs! Adding vocabulary defeats Molah's purpose as a lingua franca - it only works if everyone uses the same limited vocabulary. If you need to express something not covered, combine existing verbs or use local words for specific items.

Complete verb reference

Verbs are grouped by shared roots. Notice how Molah builds new meanings by combining familiar parts — once you know a root, you already half-know its compounds.

Bo is the core of all movement. Attach Wif (place) and A (back) to get "go-place-back" = return. Attach Woom (fast) to get Bo-Woom = hurry (go-fast). Goot (finished/done/complete) stands alone as a completion marker.

Forbidden — root: Faka (forbidden / not allowed)

Faka is the moral qualifier for prohibition — stronger than Bah (bad/unpleasant) and distinct from it. Where Bah describes something unwanted, Faka describes something that must not be done. This gives Molah a clean three-tier ethical vocabulary: Low (good), Bah (bad), Faka (forbidden). Any verb compounded with Faka means doing that action in a forbidden way — e.g. Bo-Faka (trespass), Tafl-Faka (lie/perjure), Dahma-Faka (smuggle), Sys-Faka (spy) — so only the root needs to be learned.

Rows captures the act of searching through things — moving through possibilities to find something. Maki is distinct from Smakkr (make from nothing) — it means to restore something broken to its working state. Rows-Maki = "search-fix" = troubleshoot. These two fill critical gaps for any traveler: finding a place, finding help, fixing a vehicle, fixing a misunderstanding.

Goot is used whenever something has reached completion. Ya goot = I am done. It-yuhm goot = The food is finished. Goot lah! = All done (happily)! It can follow any action to signal its completion.

Rimba and Klara — double usage words

Rimba means both rainstorm (the weather) and crazy (a wild human state). Klara means both clear sky and sober/clear-minded — sharing a root feel with Klar (clean/wash) already in the language. Context always makes the meaning clear.

Wythar ur rimba! = The weather is stormy! / That's crazy!  •  Ya ur klara. = I am sober. / The sky is clear.  •  Rimba-rimba! = Total chaos!  •  Ham ill-ur klara. = He is not sober.

Lafa — laugh and funny

Lafa means both to laugh and funny/amusing. Ya lafa = I laugh. Ham ur lafa = They are funny. The doubling and lah ending work naturally: Lafa-lafa! = Really hilarious! Lafa-lah! = So funny (warmly)! Wu ur lafa-lafa lah! = This is absolutely hilarious!

Kabah-Kah and Faka-Fak — describing the ultimate and the terrible

Kabah-Kah (the ultimate knowing) describes anything supremely good, wise, fortunate, or beautiful — a person, an outcome, a piece of luck, an animal, a moment. Faka-Fak (the utterly forbidden) describes anything truly evil, terrible, or wretched. These are descriptive words, not theological ones — Molah carries no religious affiliation.

Wythar ur kabah-kah zora! = The weather is glorious today!  •  Ham ur kabah-kah lah! = They are an incredibly wise/wonderful person!  •  Wu ur faka-fak bah. = This is a truly terrible situation.  •  Faka-fak wythar! = What awful weather!

Ham-Zuh and Wuz-Zuh — slang included

Ham-Zuh means new, but in casual speech it doubles as slang for "Cool!" or "Awesome!" — something exciting and fresh. Wuz-Zuh means old, but in casual speech it expresses disappointment — "Bummer!" or a mild expletive when something goes wrong. Molah's slang lives inside its vocabulary: no extra words needed, just a shift of tone.

Examples: Ham-Zuh lah! = That's so cool!  •  Wuz-Zuh bah! = What a bummer!  •  Sysi-krul korby ur ham-zuh. = The blue car is new.

Hospital essentials

These three words are critical for medical situations. Ur-Hama = doctor. Ur-Hamma-Wif = hospital (be-care-place). Ur-Amah-Ta = "be-help-give" = medicine. Barra-koosh = allergic (standalone — no compound logic, just a dedicated word for a vital concept).

Lesson 10: Colors in Molah

Colors in Molah are built from the verb Sys (to see). Every color is literally "a way of seeing" — which reflects the idea that color is about perception. There are five primary color words. All other colors are made by combining two of them.

The five primary colors

Sysi-ootl sis-ee-ootl Black
Sysi-mahl sis-ee-mahl Red
Sysi-bolee sis-ee-boh-lee Green (bolee)
Sysi-krul sis-ee-krul Blue

Colors as adjectives

Color words go before the noun they describe, following Molah's standard adjective-before-noun rule. Sysi-mahl tam = red thing. Sysi-krul wif = blue place. For mixed colors, simply blend the roots: Sysi-bolee-krul = yellow (green+blue, sounds like one flowing word). The Sysi- prefix is a deliberate variation of Sys (to see) — close enough to remind you of its root, distinct enough to never be confused with the verb Sys in a sentence.

To describe a color between two primaries, simply blend the two color roots together into one compound word. No special sound or separator needed — the words flow together as one.

Lesson 11: Numbers in Molah

Molah speakers have two options for numbers: use the local language's numbers (always acceptable in Molah — practicality comes first), or use the Molah number system. Both are valid. The Molah system is described here for those who want a fully self-contained language experience.

The Mr system

Mr (pronounced "Murh" — like the word myrrh, the fragrant resin) is the number root — it means "one" or signals that a number follows. Powers of ten are built by adding vowels. Individual digits 2–9 are built by adding consonants. Combine them to make any number.

Combining numbers with [!]

The tongue click [!] separates number segments — just as it means "and" everywhere else in Molah. State the largest unit first, click, then the next unit. The listener simply adds them up. This keeps each component clearly distinct and easy to parse.

The rule

To express a multi-digit number: say the largest unit, click [!], say the next unit, click again, and so on down to the smallest. For multiples of ten (20, 30, 40...) place the digit before the ten: Mr-B-A = two-ten = 20. Then click and add the ones: Mr-B-A [!] Mr-K = 25.

Writing numbers

Molah speakers may also write numbers using Roman numerals (I, V, X, L, C, D, M) or standard Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3...) — both are universally understood and perfectly acceptable in written Molah. The Mr system is primarily for spoken use when no shared numeral system is available.

Zero — [~]

Zero is written and spoken as [~] — the same teeth-suck sound that means "but" or "however." In context, the meaning is always clear: as a number it stands alone or alongside other number units.

Lesson 12: Making negatives with Ill-

To make any verb or adjective negative in Molah, simply add the prefix Ill- directly before it. This is the only negation rule in Molah — no irregular forms, no exceptions.

Ill- + [verb or adjective] = not / don't / cannot

Ill- vs Illi (lose)

Ill- is always hyphenated and attached to another word. Illi is the standalone verb meaning "to lose." They are never confused in context — Ill- is a prefix, Illi is a complete word. The hyphen makes the difference visible in writing.

Core negation examples

Positive Negative Translation
Ya kabah. Ya ill-kabah. I don't know.
Ya kab. Ya ill-kab. I don't understand.
Ya bo. Ya ill-bo. I cannot go. / I am not going.
Ya bama raz-itah. Ya ill-bama raz-itah. I don't want to sit down.
Yuhm. Ill-yuhm. Not good.
Ya tamala yuhm. Ya tamala ill-yuhm. I think it is not good. (I feel not-good)
Ya bama it. Ya ill-bama it. I don't want to eat.
Ham ur wu. Ham ill-ur wu. He/She is not here.

Ill- with adjectives and states

Molah Translation
Ill-yuhm Not good (already used naturally in conversations)
Ill-bah Not bad (so-so)
Ill-tami Not sick / feeling well
Ill-woom Not fast / slow
Ill-faka Not forbidden / allowed

Ill- with Bah-t

In Molah, Bah-t means "no" as a standalone response. Ill- is used within a sentence to negate a specific word. Think of Bah-t as "No!" and Ill- as "not" or "don't." You wouldn't say "Ill-" alone — attach it to something.

Longer sentence examples

Ya ill-kabah zee-kahb ya ill-tafl Molah yuhm-yuhm.
(yah ill-kah-bah zee-kahb yah ill-tafl moe-lah yoom-yoom)
I don't know because I don't speak Molah very well.
Ya ill-bama brim zee-kahb ya ill-tami.
(yah ill-bah-mah brim zee-kahb yah ill-tah-mee)
I don't want a drink because I am not sick.
Ham ill-ur wu [~] ham ill-bo-ko.
(hahm ill-ur woo [~] hahm ill-boh-koh)
He is not here but he did not go either.
Ya tamala ill-yuhm zee-kahb wythar ur bah-bah.
(yah tah-mah-lah ill-yoom zee-kahb wy-thar ur bah-bah)
I think it is not good because the weather is really bad.
Ya ill-bama raz-itah — ya bama bo sysi-wif!
(yah ill-bah-mah raz-ih-tah — yah bah-mah boh sis-ee-wif)
I don't want to sit down — I want to go to the movie theater!

Negating tenses

Ill- works with all tenses. The tense suffix stays on the verb, Ill- stays in front:

Tense Positive Negative Meaning
Present Ya bo. Ya ill-bo. I don't go.
Past Ya bo-ko. Ya ill-bo-ko. I did not go.
Future Ya bo-tu. Ya ill-bo-tu. I will not go.

Lesson 13: Describing the world — Easy, Hard, Long, Short

Four essential descriptors round out your ability to describe experience in Molah. Each word was chosen so that its sound matches its meaning — a Molah principle called sound honesty.

Kool does double duty

Kool already existed in Molah meaning smooth. Now it extends to easy — because in Molah, easy and smooth are the same idea: a path without resistance. You can use Kool for both. Molah ur kool! = Molah is easy!

Lesson 15: Making Molah words — and your Molah name

Molah has a fixed vocabulary of core words — but the world has far more concepts than any small language can contain. Two tools handle this gracefully: borrowing local words and converting words into Molah.

Tool 1 — Use local words freely

If a word does not exist in Molah, simply use the local word in the middle of your Molah sentence. This is not a mistake — it is encouraged, especially when you are a visitor in someone else's country. The local word takes priority.

Ya bama brim wine.
(yah bah-mah brim wine)
I want to drink wine. — local word used as-is.
Wu ur loom tafl zee-kahb philosophy ur strak.
(woo ur loom tafl zee-kahb philosophy ur strak)
This is a long conversation because philosophy is difficult.

Tool 2 — Convert words into Molah

If you prefer to keep everything inside the Molah sound system, convert the word using three steps:

The conversion rule:

  1. Write the word in its normal spelling
  2. Remove any letters not in the Molah alphabet: C D E G J N P Q V X (and Y when used as a vowel sound)
  3. Pronounce what remains using Molah letter sounds

The Molah alphabet — 16 letters only

Vowels: A I O U Y  •  Consonants: B F H K L M R S T W Z
Any letter outside this set is simply removed from the converted word.

Conversion examples

Original word Letters removed Molah version Pronunciation
Santullo N Satullo sah-tul-loh
Philosophy P, P, Y (vowel) Hilosohy hih-loh-soh-ee
Wine N, E Wi wih
Horse E Hors hohrz
Bowl (none) Bowl — or use Ish (from Dish) if needed boh-wluh / ish
School C Shool shool
Bread E Bra (wait — D also removed) brah
Music C Musi moo-sih

Converted words in a sentence

Once converted, the Molah version works just like any other word. You can add tense markers, the lah ending, or Ill- prefix: Ya bama brim wi lah! = I really want a drink of wine (happily)!  •  Ham tafl-tu Hilosohy. = They will speak about philosophy.

What if the converted word sounds like an existing Molah word?

Don't worry — context usually makes the meaning clear to the listener. If someone says Ya bama bowl, the listener will understand from context whether they mean a bowl (dish) or something else. However, if the collision causes genuine confusion, simply change the word. For example, Bowl converted from English becomes Bowl in Molah — but if that causes confusion, use Ish instead (from Dish: D-i-s-h → remove D → Ish). The listener's understanding always wins.

Your Molah name

Every Molah speaker is encouraged to write and speak their own name in Molah. Apply the same rule — remove non-Molah letters, pronounce what remains with Molah sounds. Your Molah name becomes part of your identity as a speaker.

Ya ur Satullo. Wu-wu, yo ur?
(yah ur sah-tul-loh. woo-woo, yoh ur?)
I am Satullo. Where are you from? (or: What is your name?)

Try it now

Write your name. Remove any C D E G J N P Q V X letters. What remains is your Molah name. Say it aloud using Molah sounds. This is one of the first things Molah speakers share with each other — Ya ur [your Molah name]!

Lesson 16: Why Molah is easier than any other language

Molah is designed to be the world's easiest language to learn for cross-cultural communication. Here's how it compares to other "easy" languages:

Molah vs. World Languages

Feature Molah Esperanto English Mandarin
Letters/Characters 16 letters 28 letters 26 letters ~3,000 characters
Core vocabulary to learn ~200 words ~2,000 words ~3,000 words ~2,000 words
Verb conjugations 2 (ko/tu) 6 tenses 12+ tenses None (but complex aspect)
Irregular verbs 0 0 ~200 N/A
Genders 0 0 0 0
Word order Fixed S-V-O Flexible Fixed S-V-O S-V-O
Learning time 2-3 weeks 3-6 months 6-24 months 24+ months

Key advantages of Molah

1. Phonetic clarity

Every letter has exactly ONE sound with a distinct mouth position. No confusion between similar sounds like 'b' and 'p' or 'l' and 'r'. Visual learners can literally see the difference by watching the speaker's mouth.

2. Zero exceptions

Unlike English (I go/I went), Spanish (ser/estar both mean "to be"), or French (17 verb conjugations), Molah has ZERO grammatical exceptions. Every verb follows the same pattern: add -ko for past, -tu for future. Always.

3. Minimal vocabulary

80 core words handle all essential communication. Compare to Esperanto's 2,000+ vocabulary requirement or English's thousands of irregularly formed words. You can communicate effectively with just a few hundred Molah words.

4. No cultural baggage

Unlike English (colonial history), Mandarin (Chinese dominance), or Spanish (Latin American politics), Molah belongs to no nation or culture. It's neutral by design.

5. Practical focus

Molah isn't trying to be a complete language for poetry, philosophy, or technical discourse. It's designed specifically for: buying things, asking directions, making friends, basic needs, travel situations. This narrow focus makes it incredibly efficient.

6. Self-limiting design

The rule "do not add new verbs" ensures Molah stays learnable. Every speaker uses the same 80 core words, making mutual understanding guaranteed. Other constructed languages grow unwieldy as speakers add vocabulary.

7. Systematic intensification

Instead of memorizing "good/better/best" or "fast/faster/fastest", just double the word: yuhm/yuhm-yuhm, woom/woom-woom. One simple pattern handles all intensity.

8. Time system consistency

The same -ko/-tu pattern works for verbs AND time words. Learn it once, use it everywhere: bo/bo-ko/bo-tu (go/went/will go), zora/zora-ko/zora-tu (today/yesterday/tomorrow).
